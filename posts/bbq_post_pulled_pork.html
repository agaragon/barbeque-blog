ü•© Selecting Your Pork Shoulder

Pulled pork starts with choosing the right cut. You want a bone-in pork shoulder (also called Boston butt or pork butt), weighing 8-10 pounds. The bone adds flavor and helps retain moisture during the long cook.

What to Look For:

  • Marbling: Good fat distribution throughout
  • Size: 8-10 pounds for optimal cook time
  • Color: Pink-red meat, avoid gray or brown areas
  • Fat cap: Some exterior fat is good, but trim excess

üßÇ The Perfect Pork Rub

A good rub penetrates the meat and forms a beautiful bark during cooking. Apply it at least 2 hours before cooking, but overnight is even better.

Championship Pork Rub:

  • 1/4 cup brown sugar
  • 2 tablespoons paprika
  • 2 tablespoons salt
  • 1 tablespoon black pepper
  • 1 tablespoon chili powder
  • 1 tablespoon cumin
  • 1 tablespoon garlic powder
  • 1 tablespoon onion powder
  • 1 teaspoon cayenne pepper
  • 1 teaspoon mustard powder

Mix thoroughly and apply generously to all surfaces of the pork shoulder. The sugar will help create a beautiful mahogany bark.

üî• The Low and Slow Process

Pork shoulder is incredibly forgiving, making it perfect for beginners. The high fat and connective tissue content means it's hard to overcook.

Smoking Timeline:

  • Temperature: 225-250¬∞F throughout
  • Time: 1.5-2 hours per pound
  • Target temp: 203-205¬∞F internal
  • Rest time: 1-2 hours wrapped in towels

üå°Ô∏è The Stall and How to Handle It

Around 160-170¬∞F, your pork will hit "the stall" - where internal temperature plateaus for hours. This is normal! Moisture evaporation creates cooling that balances the heat input.

Stall Management Options:

  • Wait it out: Be patient, it will eventually push through
  • Texas Crutch: Wrap in foil with some liquid
  • Butcher paper: Wrap for faster cooking while maintaining bark
  • Increase heat: Bump temperature to 275¬∞F

üåØ Wrapping Techniques

Wrapping can help power through the stall while keeping meat moist. Each method has its benefits:

Foil Wrapping (Texas Crutch):

  • Creates steam for faster cooking
  • Keeps meat very moist
  • Can soften bark significantly
  • Add butter, brown sugar, or apple juice

Butcher Paper Wrapping:

  • Breathes better than foil
  • Maintains better bark texture
  • Still speeds up cooking
  • More authentic BBQ competition style

üéØ Testing for Doneness

Temperature is important, but texture tells the real story with pulled pork:

Doneness Tests:

  • Probe test: Thermometer should slide in like butter
  • Bone wiggle: Bone should wiggle freely in the meat
  • Fork test: Fork should twist easily in the meat
  • Internal temp: 203-205¬∞F for optimal pulling

‚úã The Crucial Rest Period

Don't skip the rest! This allows juices to redistribute and the meat to finish cooking with residual heat. Wrap in towels and place in a cooler for 1-2 hours.

üç¥ Pulling and Serving

Remove the bone and any large fat chunks, then start pulling. Use two forks, meat claws, or clean hands (with gloves) to shred the meat along the grain.

Pulling Tips:

  • Pull while warm for easier shredding
  • Save the cooking juices for moisture
  • Remove excess fat as you pull
  • Mix bark pieces throughout for flavor
  • Season lightly with reserved rub if needed

Traditional:

ü•ó Perfect Coleslaw Recipe

No pulled pork sandwich is complete without coleslaw. This tangy slaw cuts through the rich pork perfectly:

Classic BBQ Slaw:

  • 1 head cabbage, finely shredded
  • 2 carrots, grated
  • 1/2 cup mayonnaise
  • 2 tablespoons apple cider vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon sugar
  • 1 teaspoon celery seed
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Mix dressing ingredients, toss with vegetables, and let sit for 30 minutes before serving.

‚ùÑÔ∏è Storage and Reheating

Pulled pork keeps well and actually improves in flavor over time:

  • Refrigerator: 3-4 days in sealed container
  • Freezer: Up to 6 months in freezer bags
  • Reheating: Low heat with added liquid (apple juice or broth)
  • Serving tip: Reheat only what you'll use

üö´ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Cooking too fast: High heat makes tough, dry meat
  • Not resting: Skipping rest results in dry, less flavorful meat
  • Under-seasoning: Large cuts need generous seasoning
  • Overcooking: Even forgiving pork can be overdone
  • Poor timing: Allow extra time, you can always hold it warm

üèÜ Competition Tips

Want to take your pulled pork to the next level? Here are some competition secrets:

  • Inject with apple juice and butter mixture
  • Use multiple wood types for complex flavor
  • Pull only the best, most tender pieces for judging
  • Keep it warm and moist in holding containers
  • Present with a variety of textures (some chunky, some fine)

üéâ Final Thoughts

Pulled pork is the gateway drug to serious barbecue. It's forgiving enough for beginners but offers plenty of room for refinement as your skills develop. The smell of pork shoulder slowly transforming over 12+ hours is one of life's great pleasures.

Don't be discouraged if your first attempt isn't perfect. Each cook teaches you something new about temperature control, timing, and flavor development. Keep notes, experiment with different rubs and woods, and most importantly, enjoy the process.

There's something magical about turning a tough, inexpensive cut of meat into something that brings people together. Fire up that smoker, grab a pork shoulder, and get ready to create some delicious memories!
